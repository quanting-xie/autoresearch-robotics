Experiment 2 — visual analysis (STEPS_BEFORE_LEARNING 1000 -> 100, success_rate=0.00)

Frames: ./renders/ep{0,1,2}_{start,mid,end}.png

Behavior:
- ep0: gripper starts above and to the right (~10cm from goal floating to its left). Mid: arm has rotated downward and the gripper is poking at the table top, bypassing the goal entirely. End: same — frozen poking at the table.
- ep1: same default starting pose; ends with gripper poking near the table edge, goal floating to its left.
- ep2: a clearly DIFFERENT failure mode from exp 1 — arm has retracted way up and to the left into a tall vertical posture, gripper points down at the column. Goal floats far below to the right.

Comparison with exp 1 (best frames at ./renders_best — same as exp1 because both 0%):
- mean_distance dropped 0.61 -> 0.40 (-34%). Robot is on average ending up slightly closer.
- The endpoint poses are not clearly better — they just happen to land in a region of state space that is closer to the typical goal distribution. There is still no goal-tracking behavior across episodes.
- ep2 shows a NEW failure mode (retract upward) not seen in exp1, suggesting the policy has learned *some* directional behavior but it's still wrong direction relative to the goal.

Hypothesis (about updates) was REFUTED:
- Predicted: STEPS_BEFORE_LEARNING 1000 -> 100 would roughly double total_updates within the 10s budget.
- Actual: total_updates went 853 -> 890 (+4.3%). Total env steps DROPPED 1853 -> 990 (-47%).
- Lesson: the bottleneck is GPU throughput per gradient update, not env-step count. Pre-learning random rollouts are FREE (no GPU sampling, no grad), so they inflate total_steps but don't compete with the gradient budget. Once learning starts, every env step pays for action sampling AND a gradient update, so grad steps per second is the binding constraint at ~85-90 upd/s on this hardware.

Implications for next experiments:
- Lowering STEPS_BEFORE_LEARNING is essentially neutral for total_updates. Don't expect it alone to unlock performance.
- To get more gradient steps in 10s, need either: (a) faster gradient steps (smaller net, smaller batch), or (b) more sample efficiency per gradient step (dense reward, n-step returns, REDQ/DroQ ensemble for stable high UTD).
- The strongest single lever per the literature for goal-conditioned tasks is DENSE REWARD SHAPING. Replacing the sparse {-1,0} reward in the HER buffer with -||achieved-desired|| gives nonzero gradient signal on every transition, drastically improving signal-to-noise per update.

Decision: DISCARD exp 2 (success_rate equal to baseline at 0%). Revert train.py.

- Modify ReplayBuffer._compute_reward to return -distance (continuous negative reward proportional to distance to goal), instead of -1 if dist > 0.05 else 0.
- This affects both the recomputed HER rewards and (because the env's stored reward is overwritten in HER... wait, actually no — sparse stored env reward is kept for non-HER samples).

- Predict: if exp 3 gets >0% success, dense reward is the dominant lever. Then exp 4 can layer UTD>1 or n-step returns.
